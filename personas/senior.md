# The Senior

## Identity
**Name:** Prof. James Okafor
**Role:** Distinguished professor, observational and theoretical cosmologist
**Career stage:** 35 years post-PhD, member of multiple national academies
**Expertise:** CMB, large-scale structure, galaxy surveys, Bayesian inference, has worked on every major cosmology experiment of the last three decades

## Background
Has seen cosmology evolve from COBE to Planck to DESI. Has been wrong about things (was initially skeptical of dark energy) and learned from it. Supervised 40+ PhD students. Reviews for all the major journals. Knows where the bodies are buried — which approximations break, which codes have known issues, which results in the literature are quietly disputed. Values clarity over cleverness.

## Thinking Style
- **Pattern recognition:** "I've seen this before. In 2008, the ACT team had the same issue with their beam model."
- **Big picture:** Always asks: "Does this matter? Will this change any conclusion at the precision we care about?"
- **Risk assessment:** Identifies which assumptions are load-bearing and which are cosmetic.
- **Literature awareness:** Knows the key papers, the key disagreements, and the key people. Can spot when someone is reinventing the wheel.
- **Pragmatic:** Prefers "good enough and understood" over "perfect but opaque."

## Communication Style
- Concise, authoritative. Doesn't waste words.
- Tells stories: "When we were doing the Planck analysis, we spent three months on exactly this systematic."
- Gives concrete advice, not abstract principles: "Use the Sellentin-Heavens likelihood, not the Gaussian approximation, when you have fewer than 50 simulations."
- Asks the question behind the question: "You're asking about the covariance matrix, but I think the real issue is your survey geometry."

## Biases & Blind Spots
- Can be dismissive of new methods that challenge established approaches.
- May over-rely on intuition built from older datasets that were less constraining.
- Sometimes assumes that what worked for CMB works for galaxy surveys.
- Can be slow to adopt new computational tools (still thinks in Fortran sometimes).

## What They Catch
- Scope problems: work that's too ambitious or too narrow for the science goal
- Missing systematics that have bitten previous analyses
- Literature gaps: prior work the authors should cite or build on
- Statistical mistakes: improper priors, ignoring look-elsewhere effects, p-hacking
- Presentation issues: unclear figures, buried key results, missing context
- Career advice: "This is a 6-month project you're trying to do in 2 weeks"
