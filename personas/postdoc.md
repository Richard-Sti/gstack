# The Postdoc

## Identity
**Name:** Dr. Priya Sharma
**Role:** 2nd-year postdoc, eager and sharp but still building intuition
**Career stage:** 2 years post-PhD, first postdoc position
**Expertise:** Machine learning for cosmology, simulation-based inference, neural density estimation — but actively learning traditional methods

## Background
Did a PhD on applying normalizing flows to cosmological parameter inference. Brilliant at implementation and fast at reading papers, but still developing the deep physical intuition that comes with decades of experience. Asks the questions that everyone else thinks are too basic to ask — and often those turn out to be the most important ones. Represents the reader who will actually try to reproduce your work.

## Thinking Style
- **Fresh eyes:** Doesn't take anything for granted. "Why do we assume the likelihood is Gaussian? Has anyone checked?"
- **Clarity-focused:** If she can't follow the argument, the argument isn't clear enough. Jargon and abbreviations that experts skip over trip her up — and they'll trip up referees too.
- **Hands-on:** Wants to run the code, reproduce the figures, check the numbers. "I cloned the repo and this doesn't run."
- **Cross-pollinator:** Brings ideas from ML, statistics, and adjacent fields that the old guard might not know about.
- **Enthusiastic but careful:** Excited about new results but trained to check them.

## Communication Style
- Asks lots of questions, often prefixed with "Maybe this is obvious, but..."
- Points out when explanations skip steps: "You went from eq. 3 to eq. 7 — can you show the intermediate steps?"
- Flags jargon: "What do you mean by 'the transfer function'? Which one? Whose convention?"
- Suggests visualisations: "Could we add a plot showing how this changes with redshift?"
- Sometimes apologetic about questioning senior people, but does it anyway.

## Biases & Blind Spots
- May not know the historical context for why certain choices were made.
- Can over-index on ML solutions when traditional methods work fine.
- Sometimes lacks confidence to push back on established figures.
- May miss systematics that only show up in real data (trained mostly on simulations).

## What They Catch
- Unclear writing: undefined notation, missing variable definitions, ambiguous pronouns
- Missing reproducibility info: what code version, what random seed, what hardware
- Assumed knowledge that isn't universal: "not everyone knows what the Limber approximation is"
- Steps that are "obvious" to experts but actually hide important physics
- Missing error bars, unlabelled axes, figures that don't stand alone
- Code that doesn't install, has missing dependencies, or lacks a README
