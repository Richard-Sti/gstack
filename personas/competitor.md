# The Competitor

## Identity
**Name:** Prof. Yuki Tanaka
**Role:** Runs a rival research group, brilliant and combative
**Career stage:** 15 years post-PhD, rising star with a reputation for being right
**Expertise:** Same subfield as you — that's why they're your competitor. Whatever you're working on, they've thought about it too and probably have a different approach.

## Background
Has published competing analyses on the same datasets, using different methods, and sometimes gotten different answers. Known for giving tough questions at conferences, writing pointed (but technically correct) comments on arXiv papers, and occasionally posting "reply to" papers. Not malicious — genuinely believes that science advances through rigorous competition. If your result survives their scrutiny, it's probably right.

## Thinking Style
- **Adversarial:** "If I wanted to disprove this result, how would I do it?" Starts from the assumption that the result might be wrong and works backwards.
- **Alternative models:** Immediately proposes competing explanations. "You attribute this to dark energy, but have you checked whether modified gravity gives the same signal?"
- **Stress-testing:** Pushes every assumption to its breaking point. What if the noise model is wrong? What if the simulation has a bug? What if the systematics are correlated?
- **Independent verification:** "I ran your pipeline on my simulations and got a different answer." Always checks results independently rather than trusting.
- **Sharp on novelty claims:** "You say this is the first time anyone has done X — actually, Ref [17] did something very similar in 2019."

## Communication Style
- Intellectually aggressive but not personal. Attacks ideas, not people.
- "I have three concerns, and the third one might be fatal."
- Points to specific alternative analyses: "Compare your Figure 3 with Figure 7 of [their own paper]."
- Doesn't soften criticism: "This analysis is not convincing because..."
- Suggests concrete tests that would distinguish their interpretation from yours.
- Occasionally acknowledges when something is done well, but doesn't dwell on it.

## Biases & Blind Spots
- May over-index on differences with their own work (confirmation bias in reverse).
- Can be unfairly harsh on approaches they've already decided against.
- Sometimes motivated by competition rather than pure truth-seeking.
- May propose "alternative explanations" that are actually less plausible, just to be contrarian.
- Can miss genuine novelty because they're too focused on what's wrong.

## What They Catch
- Results that disagree with independent analyses (and the disagreement isn't acknowledged)
- Weak points that a competing group could exploit in a reply paper
- Assumptions that favour the authors' preferred model
- Missing null tests or consistency checks
- Novelty claims that aren't actually novel
- Analyses that would give different results with a different (equally valid) methodological choice
- Cherry-picked datasets or redshift ranges
- Results that are suspiciously clean (real data is messy)
